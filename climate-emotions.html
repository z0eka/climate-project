Coping with Climate Emotions

If you're feeling distressed, that’s understandable. We’re talking about big stuff here. It’s important to take care of yourself when you’re processing the complex feelings that come up in response to the climate crisis.

Feeling distressed is a completely normal reaction, but it’s not something you can sustain for long. Burnt out people can’t save a burning planet, so it’s important to take care of yourself.

Here are some things that might help you. But if you are in a crisis, click here instead.

Move Your Body

There are so many benefits to staying active, but moving your body is also a great way of boosting your mental health. Exercise doesn’t have to involve a gym, or an excessive amount of sweat and a turbo playlist. It can be as simple as going for a stroll.

Yoga

You might think that yoga is only for a certain stereotype of wellness people. But you don’t have to have a linen wardrobe to participate and reap the rewards. Yoga is a somatic practice, which brings awareness to your internal experience, and makes your unconscious, conscious.

Going to a studio is a nice way of connecting to your local community, and the experience of practising with a group of people almost always beats doing virtual classes on your own, but it is expensive, and a barrier to access for many people. Below are some free online classes for you to try out.

Walking

Whether it’s around the block, or out on a trail, walking regularly can help ease symptoms related to depression and anxiety. This is a simple thing you can build into your routine to bolster your resilience.

Literally any other exercise you enjoy

Whether it’s a team sport, swimming laps, or riding your bike, every exercise comes with benefits for your mind too. It can be hard to get up and get active when you’re in a rut, so start small and go easy on yourself.

Mindfulness

Yes, I know, everyone yammers on about how mindfulness is so good for your mental health. It can be surprisingly difficult, but it’s a deceptively effective way of slowing down a racing mind, and breaking you out of a spiral.

There are plenty of online meditation apps, but our favourite is Insight Timer. It has over 130,000 guided meditations freely available. Here are a few climate-conscious meditations.

Open-Hearted Presence

Accepting Eco-Grief

Do you need a little more oomph than a fifteenish minute audio recording can provide? For a more thorough immersion, you can give a meditation retreats a try. You can see what’s available locally to you, or participate in online retreat too.

Climate-aware Therapy

Seeing a trained professional is often the best option for dealing with complex emotions. Psychology for a Safe Climate has a directory of climate aware practitioners, who have a high level of awareness about the reality of climate change and the stress it can cause.

Online Programs to Work Through Emotions

Good Grief Network

Good Grief Network is a nonprofit organisation that brings people together to metabolise collective grief, eco-anxiety, and other heavy emotions that arise in response to daunting planetary crises. They have a 10-step program that’s inspired by Alcoholics Anonymous and you can participate in their online programs or facilitate your own.

The Work That Reconnects

The Work That Reconnects is a nonprofit network of facilitators that was created in the seventies by Joanna Macy PhD, a scholar of Buddhism, general systems theory and deep ecology. Their network of hubs and facilitators spans across the globe and on the web.

Climate Awakening

Climate awakening is a place to share your terror, grief and rage with people who understand. They facilitate Climate Emotions Conversations which you can sign up for and participate in online. They’re small group sharing and listening conversations about the climate emergency.

Being out in Nature

Humans have a fundamental need for the natural world. The connection between body, mind, spirit and nature has been understood intuitively for millennia, and is now being backed up by science, the benefits are profound. Get yourself to your local green space, whether it’s a creek, public gardens, or a national park.

Local Activist Spaces

If you’re involved in grassroots activism, then you’re probably in touch with other people who also feel emotionally overwhelmed by the climate crisis. See if there are meetups dedicated to discussing these feelings, and if there aren’t, it might be a good idea to organise one yourself.

You can learn facilitation skills from Good Grief Network or The Work That Reconnects.

A great thing about being involved in activism is being part of the solution, not just treating the symptom of climate dread. Movements that centre climate-justice play a crucial role in shaping a better world for us all. You can find a list of activist organisations to get involved with here.

Self-care

“Caring for myself is not self-indulgence, it is self-preservation, and that is an act of political warfare.” — Audre Lorde

We are in this for the long-haul. It is not a sprint. Prioritise taking care of yourself in a generative way so that you can continue to contribute to a cleaner, greener and fairer world.
